THE QUANTUM MECHANICAL MODEL

1911 - Niels Bohr, “atom could be pictured as a small positive nucleus with electrons orbiting around it”–the Bohr model

mid 1920s - Bohr model was incorrect, Louis Victor De Broglie (France) and Erwin Schrodinger (Austria) suggested that electron might also exhibit wave and particle characteristics

Wave Mechanical Model

Schrodinger carried out a mathematical analysis that led to a new model

Wave function or atomic orbital - mathematical description of electron’s motion

Electron cloud/density - squaring the orbital = high probability of finding that electron

Quantum Numbers - required to describe each electron; orbital size, shape, and orientation

Principal QN (n) - size and energy (1, 2, 3, 4…), smaller = lower energy, closer to the nucleus, shell

Angular Momentum QN (l) - shape, value: 0 to n-1

- l = 0: s-orbital (sharp), spherical and center nucleus, greater value of n = larger sphere

- l = 1: p-orbital (principal), hourglass/dumbbell, two lobes of high electron density

- l = 2: d-orbital (diffuse), variety of shapes

- l = 3: f-orbital (fundamental), more complex shapes

Magnetic QN (ml) - describes the orientation of the orbital sound, -l to 0 to +l

Spin QN (ms) - direction of the electron spinning, +½–two electrons occupy the orbital–or -½–one, spin-paired electrons

Pauli’s Exclusion Principle - no two electrons can have the same set of the four quantum numbers

Aufbau Principle - lower energy levels must be filled first before filling the higher energy levels

Electron Configuration - the use of n and l QN and the number of electrons

s-subshell: 2 electrons p-subshell: 5 electrons d-subshell: 10 electrons f-subshell: 14 electrons Orbital Box Diagram - boxes grouped by sublevel with small arrows as electrons, complete the upward arrows before downward arrows s-orbital: 1 box, 2 electrons p-orbital: 3 boxes, 6 electrons d-orbital: 5 boxes, 10 electrons f-orbital: 7 boxes, 14 electrons
